### 💬 On the "Annual Margin Is OK So Prices Don't Need Adjusting Often" Comment

**Partially right, but missing the critical bit.**

David is right that constantly tweaking prices is disruptive — especially with ADHD, the less daily admin the better. Prices should be set to be broadly sustainable and then left alone. That principle is sound.

**But here's what it misses:**

1. **The losses on specific lines are cash losses, right now.** If Belgium strawberry is selling at £3.60 when it costs £2.25–4.00/punnet, you are handing money away on every punnet. Over a busy June weekend you might sell 30 punnets. That's £12–40 lost in two days. Annualised over the whole strawberry season that's hundreds of pounds. Annual margin being "OK" overall masks these holes.

2. **Perishables punish you for getting it wrong in-season, not at year-end.** If you price strawberries too low in June and they sell fast, you made less than you should have in the one month they sell well. You cannot go back and sell June strawberries in December. The loss is permanent.

3. **The prices that *really* need adjusting are only about 6–8 lines** (lychees, passion fruit, kiwi, grapefruit, Belgium strawberry, lemon, cucumber). These are not random tweaks — they're structural fixes to stop recurring losses. Once fixed, they can stay fixed.

4. **What he's right about:** You don't need to change prices daily. Set a good base price, don't discount, and don't fuss. The seasonal adjustment that *does* matter is to **raise** prices on premium items when they're in-season and customers expect to pay more (Belgium strawberry in June, cherries, stone fruit peak). Not to match the cost drop — to maintain the value signal.

**The one-sentence answer:** Annual margin is a lagging indicator — the damage from wrong prices happens daily. Fix the 6–8 problem lines once, then leave them alone. That's both low-admin AND commercially correct.
